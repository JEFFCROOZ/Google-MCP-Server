## Summary
The article argues that real-time product analytics are actively harmful rather than simply unnecessary, because they encourage statistically invalid decision-making — stopping A/B tests too early, ignoring significance, and reacting to noise rather than signal. For data leaders, this is a critical governance argument: the architecture of your analytics system shapes the quality of decisions made from it, and speed without statistical discipline produces worse outcomes than a 24-hour delay. The distinction between operational metrics (where real-time is appropriate) and product analytics (where it is dangerous) is a foundational design principle for any data strategy.

## Key Excerpts
> Conflating operational monitoring with product decision-making is a fatal mistake — real-time is essential for deploys, but harmful for interpreting user behavior and running experiments.
> Real-time analytics create pressure to halt experiments the moment significance appears, a practice that reliably produces false conclusions due to how significance fluctuates over time.
> Building complex real-time infrastructure without a valid decision-making use case is the worst pitfall — premature sophistication in data architecture creates risk without corresponding value.
