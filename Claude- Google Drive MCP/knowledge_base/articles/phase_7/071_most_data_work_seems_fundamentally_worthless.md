## Summary
The author argues that a large portion of data work—particularly in public sector and large organizations—produces no measurable value, not due to individual failure but because organizations use data initiatives primarily as performative signaling rather than operational improvement. This matters for data leaders because it exposes a systemic incentive misalignment: stakeholders demand "data-driven" optics while defaulting to spreadsheets and ignoring actual outputs, making governance and impact measurement structurally difficult. Understanding this pattern is essential for data leaders trying to justify investment, build credible teams, and avoid building infrastructure nobody uses.

## Key Excerpts
> Many data teams exist primarily to signal organizational commitment to being "data-driven," not to produce decisions or outcomes that generate real value.
> Stakeholder behavior reveals the misalignment: the same leaders demanding urgent data deliverables never check dashboards and remain unaware of significant errors in the work.
> The social norm in dysfunctional data teams becomes quiet acceptance—employees collectively abandon ambitions for meaningful work and optimize instead for comfort and job preservation.
