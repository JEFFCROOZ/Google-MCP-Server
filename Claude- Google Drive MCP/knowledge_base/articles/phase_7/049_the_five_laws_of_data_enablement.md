## Summary
Drawing on S.R. Ranganathan's 1931 *Five Laws of Library Science*, the article argues that data teams should orient around **enablement over governance** — prioritizing broad, democratized access to data rather than protecting or restricting it. The analogy to librarianship reframes the data team's mission: not as custodians of a technical asset, but as user-centric partners who make data findable, usable, and trustworthy for everyone in the organization. For data leaders, this provides a principled framework for staffing, stakeholder engagement, and organizational design that justifies the team's value in business terms.

## Key Excerpts
> When forced to choose between data governance and data enablement, data teams should optimize for enablement — storage and processing only matter insofar as they drive actual use.
> Democratization requires active infrastructure: simply opening access without curation, sponsorship, and federated support replicates the "Little Free Library" failure mode, where desirable data stays siloed.
> Data team hiring should prioritize personality, tact, and stakeholder empathy alongside technical skill, positioning the team as a "friend, philosopher, and guide" to the organization.
