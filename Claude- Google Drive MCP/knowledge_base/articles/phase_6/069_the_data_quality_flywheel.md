## Summary
The article argues that data quality is not a one-time fix but a self-reinforcing cycle: the more stakeholders actively use data, the faster issues are surfaced and resolved, creating compounding improvements over time. For data leaders, this reframes data quality as an organizational culture problem rather than a technical one — success depends on building broad data adoption first, then leveraging that usage to drive quality accountability across the business, not just within the data team.

## Key Excerpts
> Data quality has natural entropy that can never be fully controlled; the goal is building systems resilient to persistent degradation, not achieving a perfect end state.
> Broader data usage creates stronger organizational incentives to fix quality issues — if no one uses the data, no one is motivated to maintain it.
> Data leaders should prioritize the most critical business KPIs first to establish a minimum quality baseline before attempting to scale trust across the organization.
