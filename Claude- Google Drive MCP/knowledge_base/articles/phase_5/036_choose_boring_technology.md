## Summary
Dan McKinley argues that organizations have a limited number of "innovation tokens" to spend on novel or experimental choices, and wasting them on infrastructure novelty (new databases, programming paradigms) undermines capacity to innovate where it matters most. For data leaders, this reframes technology selection as a strategic resource allocation problem: defaulting to well-understood, mature data tools preserves organizational attention for higher-order business challenges rather than operational firefighting.

## Key Excerpts
> Organizations have only a few "innovation tokens" to spend; using them on experimental databases or infrastructure leaves none for genuine business innovation.
> Mature, battle-tested software requires less operational care than newly released tools, making reliability a compounding advantage over time.
> Developers' desire for novel technologies often reflects personal enthusiasm rather than the highest path to organizational or product success.
