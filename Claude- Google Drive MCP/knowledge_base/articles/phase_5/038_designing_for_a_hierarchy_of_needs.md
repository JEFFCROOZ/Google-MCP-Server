## Summary
The article adapts Maslow's hierarchy of needs to product and UX design, proposing a five-level framework—functionality, reliability, usability, proficiency, and creativity—where each level must be sufficiently met before higher-level value can be delivered. For data leaders, this hierarchy offers a useful mental model for prioritizing data product development: data systems must first be functional and reliable before investing in advanced analytics, self-service capabilities, or innovative features. It also acknowledges, via Max-Neef's critique, that needs can be pursued simultaneously with trade-offs, which reflects the reality of iterative data platform building.

## Key Excerpts
> A design must achieve basic functionality before any higher-level qualities matter—failing at the foundation renders all other features irrelevant.
> The five-level design hierarchy moves from functionality and reliability through usability and proficiency, culminating in creativity as the highest-order achievement.
> An alternative model suggests needs can be pursued simultaneously with trade-offs, rather than strictly in sequence, challenging rigid hierarchical prioritization.
