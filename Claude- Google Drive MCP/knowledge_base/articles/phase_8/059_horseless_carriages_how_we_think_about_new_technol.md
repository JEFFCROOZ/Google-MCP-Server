## Summary
The article argues that many AI applications fail not because AI models are insufficient, but because they are designed around legacy interaction paradigms ("horseless carriages") that constrain what AI can do. For data leaders, this distinction matters: the difference between AI as a tacked-on feature versus AI as a re-architected workflow engine determines whether AI investments deliver real productivity gains or merely create the illusion of innovation.

## Key Excerpts
> Poorly designed AI apps mimic old software patterns, unnecessarily limiting powerful models rather than reimagining workflows around what AI can actually do.
> The Gmail AI draft example shows that bad AI design can make a tool slower to use than doing the task manually — a critical failure mode for any AI product.
> A superior AI email assistant reads and triages incoming messages autonomously using tools and context, rather than waiting for users to prompt it from scratch each time.
